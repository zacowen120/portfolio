Operation: Trust

Building Trust at The Motley Fool

A 30-year-old company with over a million members, and a trust problem we set out to measure and fix.

My role: Led the company's first in-house research initiative, communicated the findings to the global organization, and led a team to implement them across a 600-person company. Also ran member testimonial initiatives at events around the nation, led the member-first email program, and pioneered the member loyalty card.

Member trust up 18 percentage points a year. Email pain points down 22. And a handwritten card that reached members representing $1.2M in renewals.
Background

The Motley Fool has been around for 30+ years and grown to over 1M subscribing members by making great stock picks. But while membership grew, trust didn't. Years of salesy tactics cost them an immense amount of trust with members and prospects. And the public mentioned it often.

Problem

Rebuild trust with Motley Fool members and prospects. And first, figure out what "trust" even means to them.

Process

Led a 2-year research project, steering a cross-functional team across brand, BI, UX, marketing, and SEO to identify what builds and breaks trust with members. Conducted 40+ hours of interviews, surveyed thousands of members, reviewed academic frameworks, and pressure-tested the work with the EVP of Southwest Airlines and the VP of Marketing at LinkedIn. Built a framework of top-rated trust builders, presented it to the company of 600+, and worked with 15 teams to implement it.

Insight

People don't trust the company to make the stock picks. They trust the faces behind the picks: the analysts. That was the biggest driver of trust.

Strategy

Put the analysts forward in every member touchpoint possible (podcasts, video, email, articles, social, product updates) so members feel connected to the people picking their stocks. Add satisfaction metrics to emails (smiley ratings that had to stay at 50% or more smiley). Interview members at events and put their stories into the marketing.

The Findings

Two things built trust.

All that research pointed to two drivers, far above the rest.

01

Connection to the analysts.

Members don't trust the company to make the picks. They trust the faces behind them. Feeling connected to the analysts was the single biggest driver of trust.

02

Feeling that people care.

The second was simpler, and just as strong. Members who felt the company genuinely cared about them as people trusted it far more.

Underneath both, a framework we built from the research: competence, transparency, and care.

In Action, One

Videos answering the questions that matter to prospects, showing the analysts' know-how and their human side. Plus transparency when it counted, like the CIO writing members directly through a market scare.

The Email Experience

A member-first inbox.

I led a team on the member-first email initiative: rethinking what we sent and how, then rewriting email across a list of multiple millions. Satisfaction climbed past baseline, and past target, on every dimension we tracked.

where it landedbaselinetarget

Sometimes a better inbox means stopping something. We ended the Premarket Briefing to make room for messaging that actually served the member, and I wrote the announcement to land it with care.

[Member Announcement] Premarket Briefings Are Ending (For Now)

We have an important announcement: this will be the last Premarket Briefing for the foreseeable future. Building a member experience that makes you Smarter, Happier, and Richer is our goal, and we also understand the overabundance of recommendations, mindset guidance, and market commentary in your inbox can become overwhelming.

So we felt it was in the members' best interest to discontinue this additional email. Much of the same content already lives in your other emails and on your Motley Fool dashboard. In the coming weeks, we will keep improving your email experience, with the goal of delivering only the highest quality and relevance to you.

Thank you for your readership.

The note acknowledged the frustration, reinforced our purpose, and promised to keep improving. Change, handled with care.

Satisfaction

Making members smile.

Separate from the email overhaul: I put a satisfaction rating on member emails, a smiley or a frowny. The rule was simple. We only kept sending an email if it scored at least 50% smiles, while still hitting revenue targets.

Brandland
